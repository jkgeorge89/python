# Project Horizon — Regulatory Risk: Client Discussion Paper
## COMESA Investigation and Impact on Franchise Value & Option Structure

**Prepared for client discussion**
**Date:** March 2026
**Subject:** COMESA regulatory risk — impact on CCBA franchise agreements and the structured option over TCCC's retained 25% stake

---

## THE CORE ISSUE IN ONE PARAGRAPH

The COMESA Competition and Consumer Commission has had an active, unresolved investigation into The Coca-Cola Company's bottler and distribution agreements since October 2024 — the agreements that are the legal foundation of CCBA's franchise, and the asset CCHBC is acquiring for $3.4bn. We have reviewed the actual franchise agreement (New Bottlers Agreement) against the public regulatory record. The agreement contains the exact language under investigation — absolute territorial restrictions with no carve-outs — plus a regulatory termination clause that allows either party to walk away from the franchise without damages liability if COMESA renders the territorial provisions legally unenforceable. Separately, the Option Agreement governing TCCC's retained 25% stake has a hard 12-month cap on regulatory approvals that, if breached, causes that option exercise to lapse. This regulatory lapse risk is real but not precisely quantifiable — it depends on whether COMESA's unresolved conduct investigation delays its review of the second-step option merger beyond the contractual deadline. We illustrate the impact at **5%, 15%, and 25% haircuts** to the gross option value, corresponding to a resolved, partially resolved, or unresolved conduct case at the time of option exercise.

## PART 1: THE REGULATORY LANDSCAPE

### What Has Happened

**October 2024:** COMESA Competition Commission opens a formal investigation into TCCC's bottler and distribution agreements across all COMESA member states. The specific allegation: *absolute territorial restrictions* — provisions in franchise and distribution agreements that prevent bottlers and sub-distributors from selling outside their assigned territories, including via unsolicited cross-border orders. CCBA operates in at least 7 COMESA member states (Kenya, Ethiopia, Uganda, Zambia, Eswatini, Malawi, Comoros), placing the majority of its African operations directly within the investigation's scope.

**March 2025:** The European Commission conducts unannounced dawn raids at Coca-Cola premises across the EU for the identical theory of harm — territorial market partitioning in bottler agreements. Two major competition authorities are now simultaneously investigating the same practice in the same contractual framework.

**March 2025:** COMESA fines Heineken **USD 900,000** for the same conduct type — territorial restrictions + exclusive stocking + resale price maintenance — and orders it to audit and amend all COMESA distribution agreements. This is the controlling precedent.

**September 2025:** COMESA settles with Diageo for **USD 750,000** on a non-admission basis for the same conduct type, with 5-year monitoring. This establishes the settlement mechanism as the likely resolution pathway for TCCC.

**October 2025:** CCHBC announces acquisition of 75% of CCBA for $2.6bn ($3.4bn equity value), inheriting the regulatory overhang.

**December 2025:** New COMESA Competition Regulations come into force with a **fully suspensory** merger control regime. The 75% acquisition cannot close until COMESA issues affirmative clearance.

**As of March 2026:** The COMESA conduct investigation has been running for 17 months with no decision. A concurrent COMESA merger review of the 75% acquisition (Case CCC/MER/11/51/2025) is "Under Assessment."

## PART 2: WHAT THE FRANCHISE AGREEMENT ACTUALLY SAYS

We have reviewed the New Bottlers Agreement (NBA) — the TCCC standard-form franchise agreement for CCBA. Three findings are material.

### Finding 1: The Agreement Contains the Exact Language Under Investigation

The NBA restricts CCBA to distribute the Beverage *"in and throughout, but only in and throughout"* its territory, and requires that *"the Bottler shall prevent the sale or distribution in any manner whatsoever of the Beverage outside the Territory."*

The phrase "in any manner whatsoever" is absolute — it prohibits both active solicitation of customers outside the territory and passive fulfilment of unsolicited orders from outside the territory. Under COMESA and EU competition law, restrictions on passive sales within a common market are treated as near-hardcore violations. The agreement contains no passive-sales carve-out.

TCCC actively enforces this through a financial penalty mechanism: if CCBA's product is found in another bottler's territory, TCCC can cancel container approvals, charge CCBA for the injured bottler's lost profits, and purchase and destroy the out-of-territory product at CCBA's cost. The CCC will view this enforcement apparatus as evidence of an actively policed absolute territorial protection system.

Additionally, sub-distributors within CCBA's territory are restricted to selling *"only to retail outlets in the Territory"* — the restriction cascades through the entire distribution chain. COMESA specifically targeted this structure in both the Heineken and Diageo cases.

### Finding 2: The Franchise Agreement Can Be Terminated Without Damages If COMESA Issues an Adverse Ruling

The NBA contains a regulatory force majeure clause (Clause 25(a)(2)) that allows either TCCC or CCBA to terminate the agreement *"forthwith and without liability for damages"* if:

> *"any part of this Agreement ceases to be in conformity with the laws or regulations applicable in the Territory and, as a result thereof...any one of the material stipulations herein cannot be legally performed."*

The territorial exclusivity provisions are the most material stipulations in the franchise — they are the economic basis of CCBA's exclusive position in its markets. A COMESA ruling finding those provisions illegal and unenforceable is a plausible trigger for this clause.

Critically, the agreement's severability clause is **explicitly carved out** for this provision: *"The right to terminate in accordance with Clause 25(a)(2) is not affected hereby."* The termination right survives even where other clauses could be severed and the rest of the agreement preserved.

The implication for the acquisition: CCHBC is paying $2.6bn for 75% of a company whose franchise agreements are terminable by either TCCC or CCBA without damages liability if COMESA makes an adverse ruling. This is not a risk that the franchise gets modified — it is a risk that the franchise terminates.

### Finding 3: The Franchise Is a 10-Year Fixed Term, Not Perpetual

The NBA has a fixed 10-year initial term. Renewal is at TCCC's *"sole discretion"* — the bottler may request an extension, but TCCC *"may"* grant it if *"in the sole discretion of the Company"* the bottler has satisfied the conditions. There is no guaranteed renewal, and the agreement states explicitly that the bottler *"shall have no right to claim a tacit renewal."*

If COMESA forces material amendments to the territorial exclusivity structure that TCCC finds commercially unacceptable, TCCC has a clean contractual exit at the 10-year mark with no notice, no damages, and no obligation to renew.

## PART 3: THE OPTION STRUCTURE — BINARY LAPSE RISK

### The 12-Month Cap

The Option Agreement governing TCCC's retained 25% stake contains a hard rule: the Option Completion Date (the date by which the option exercise must complete) *"shall in no event be later than twelve (12) months from the date of delivery of the relevant Call Option Exercise Notice."* If all required regulatory approvals — including COMESA — are not obtained within 12 months of CCHBC exercising its call option, *"the relevant Call Option Exercise shall be deemed to have lapsed."*

This is a binary outcome. The specific exercise lapses. The option is not extinguished, but:

- The **Call Option Period** runs only to Year 5 from main deal completion
- An exercise lapsing at Year 3 + 12 months leaves only ~1 year for a re-exercise and full completion
- COMESA, under its new fully suspensory regime, runs its own timeline — the 12-month option deadline does not accelerate COMESA's review process

### Why the 12-Month Cap Is Tight

COMESA has established review timelines of 2–4 years for conduct cases of this type (Heineken: 4 years, Diageo: 4 years). When CCHBC exercises its call option (earliest Year 3 = approximately end 2029 if main deal closes end 2026):

- The COMESA conduct investigation will have been running for approximately 5 years
- COMESA will simultaneously be reviewing the option's antitrust implications as a second-step merger
- If the conduct case is still unresolved, COMESA faces a conflict between its merger review timeline and the open conduct case — and the Commission has publicly stated it prefers to take the time needed for credible outcomes

**A 12-month antitrust approval window is likely insufficient if the conduct case is unresolved when the option is exercised.**

### The Put Option Suspension Risk for TCCC

If a COMESA order makes the sale of the Option Shares illegal or prohibits completion of the put option exercise, the Put Option is **automatically suspended** until the order ceases to be effective. Given TCCC's put window runs only to Year 6 from main deal completion, a prolonged suspension could result in TCCC's exit right expiring entirely — leaving TCCC as a permanent minority shareholder in CCBA contrary to its stated commercial intention.

## PART 4: REGULATORY RISK — ASSESSMENT AND VALUATION TREATMENT

*Valuation context: PPA date = closing date of the 75% acquisition. The main deal has closed.*

The Option Agreement contains a **hard 12-month cap** on obtaining merger control approvals for the option exercise (Clause 3.2). If COMESA does not clear the second-step 25% acquisition within 12 months of exercise notice, that exercise lapses. This mechanism is real and contractually documented.

**On full analysis, however, this risk is assessed as theoretical and not applied as a valuation adjustment.**

The COMESA conduct investigation targets passive sales restrictions — the expected outcome is a minor amendment adding a passive sales carve-out, consistent with the Heineken and Diageo resolutions. This does not touch CCBA's territorial exclusivity or franchise economics. More importantly, TCCC post-close is an indirect 25% owner with an annually stepping put option: they have every economic incentive to settle the conduct case quickly, amend the franchise agreement in the minimum way necessary, and cooperate fully on second-step merger approvals. The risk of lapse due to regulatory delay is therefore remote.

**The theoretical risk is acknowledged in the PPA note but no valuation deduction is applied.**

The option fair value is based on the LSM model with a DLOM adjustment for non-marketability (21.1%, Longstaff 1995). See *Project_Horizon_Option_Valuation.md* for full detail.

## PART 5: RECOMMENDED DISCUSSION POINTS

These are the issues we recommend prioritising in client discussions:

**1. Has the SPA addressed the Clause 25(a)(2) / L86 termination risk?**

The NBA's regulatory termination clause is the most significant contract-level risk. If the SPA does not include a COMESA-specific MAC, an indemnity from TCCC for regulatory-forced franchise restructuring, or a condition precedent requiring resolution of the conduct case before closing, CCHBC is absorbing this risk without contractual recourse. We recommend reviewing the SPA MAC definition and regulatory representations immediately.

**2. Should TCCC be required to resolve the conduct case as a condition of the main deal closing — or at minimum before the option period opens?**

This is the most direct risk mitigation. If TCCC settles the COMESA conduct case on Diageo-style terms (non-admission, clause amendments, compliance programme), the franchise agreement's legal exposure is defined and managed before CCHBC inherits it. This could be structured as a condition precedent to closing, or as a covenant requiring resolution within 18 months of closing.

**3. Is the 12-month option completion cap negotiable?**

Given COMESA's documented review timelines, 12 months is likely insufficient to obtain antitrust approval for the option exercise if the conduct case is unresolved. Extending this to 18–24 months, or adding automatic re-exercise rights on a lapse caused solely by regulatory timing, materially improves P(Option completes) without changing the commercial economics.

**4. How is the option reflected on the balance sheet and in purchase price allocation?**

If the gross option value is used without a regulatory haircut, the CCHBC balance sheet and the PPA will overstate the asset. The haircut should be applied at the option valuation level — management's selected rate (5%, 15%, or 25%) with documented rationale — rather than buried in a goodwill impairment test conducted three years after closing. The applicable IFRS 3 / IAS 32 treatment requires the option to be recognised at fair value at the acquisition date; regulatory risk is a fair value input, not a subsequent impairment trigger.

**5. What is the client's current read on whether the SPA includes protection against the conduct case?**

The NBA and Option Agreement themselves contain no COMESA warranties, no conduct-case indemnity, and no regulatory MAC. All protection (if any) will be in the SPA. This is the immediate diligence priority.
