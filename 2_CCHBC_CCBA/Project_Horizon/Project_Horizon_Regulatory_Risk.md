## COMESA Investigation: Impact on Franchise Agreements, Deal Structure & Option Valuation

**Classification:** Internal — Investment Committee Use Only

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The COMESA Competition Commission (now "CCCC") has an active, unresolved investigation into TCCC's bottler and distribution agreements — the same contractual framework that governs CCBA's operations and that CCHBC is acquiring for $2.6bn. This is not a peripheral regulatory risk. Having reviewed the actual franchise agreement (New Bottlers Agreement, "NBA") and the Option Agreement against the public regulatory record, we find:

1. **The NBA contains the exact language under investigation** — absolute territorial restrictions ("in and throughout, but only in and throughout") with zero passive-sales carve-outs, actively enforced by a financial penalty mechanism. This is textbook Article 16 COMESA violation territory.

2. **The NBA contains a regulatory termination clause (Clause 25(a)(2)) that allows either party to exit the franchise without damages liability** if COMESA makes the territorial restrictions legally unperformable. This is existential franchise risk, not clause modification risk.

3. **The Option Agreement's 12-month completion cap creates a binary lapse risk.** If COMESA regulatory approval for the 25% option exercise is not obtained within 12 months of notice, that exercise lapses. Given COMESA's enforcement posture and the live conduct investigation, this is a material probability event.

4. **The regulatory risk on the option exercise is theoretical and assessed as not material enough to apply as a valuation adjustment.** The COMESA conduct case is expected to result in a minor franchise amendment (passive sales carve-out) — not an impairment of core franchise economics. TCCC's 25% ownership post-close creates fully aligned incentives to settle quickly and cooperate on approvals. The second-step merger filing is expected to be routine once the conduct case is resolved.

5. **The option valuation applies a DLOM adjustment only.** The regulatory lapse risk is acknowledged here and in *Project_Horizon_Option_Valuation.md* but is not applied as a quantified deduction to the option fair value.

## SECTION 2: FRANCHISE AGREEMENT ANALYSIS — NBA CLAUSE CROSS-REFERENCE

*Document: Project Horizon — New Bottlers Agreement and Agreement.docx*

### 2.1 The Core Territorial Restriction — The Exact Language Under Investigation

**NBA Clause I — Object of the Agreement:**
> *"The Company hereby authorizes the Bottler...to prepare and package the Beverage...and to distribute and sell the same under the Trade Marks, **in and throughout, but only in and throughout**, the following territory"* **(L12)**

**NBA Clause II — Active Territorial Prohibition:**
> *"**The Bottler shall prevent the sale or distribution in any manner whatsoever of the Beverage outside the Territory.**"* **(L32)**

**Assessment:** "In any manner whatsoever" admits of no exception — it covers both active sales (bottler soliciting customers outside territory) and passive sales (unsolicited cross-border orders). Under COMESA and EU competition law, passive sales restrictions are treated as near-hardcore violations. There is **no passive-sales carve-out anywhere in the agreement**.

### 2.2 The Active Enforcement Mechanism — Confirms Conduct is Not Theoretical

**NBA Clauses II/III — Remedies for Out-of-Territory Sales (L33–38):**

If CCBA's product is found in another bottler's territory, TCCC may:
- Cancel container approvals forthwith
- Charge CCBA compensation including the injured bottler's **lost profits and expenses**
- Purchase and destroy out-of-territory product **at CCBA's cost**
- Require CCBA to assist in all investigations of out-of-territory distribution
- Require CCBA to immediately report any third-party solicitation that might lead to out-of-territory sales

**Assessment:** The CCC will view this enforcement apparatus as evidence of an actively policed absolute territorial protection system. This is not a passive contract term — it is an operational mechanism for enforcing the restriction the CCC considers illegal. This strengthens the CCC's case.

### 2.3 Cascaded Territorial Restriction — Sub-Distributors Also Locked

**NBA Clause II (L31):**
> *"the Bottler shall be authorized to distribute and sell the Beverage to wholesalers in the Territory **who sell only to retail outlets in the Territory**."*

**Assessment:** The absolute territorial restriction cascades from TCCC → CCBA → wholesalers → retailers, all locked within the territory. In the Heineken and Diageo cases, the CCC specifically targeted cascaded territorial restrictions at the distributor level. This is a double-layer violation.

### 2.4 Concerted Practice Evidence — Elevates from Vertical to Network Restriction

**NBA Clause II (L28–29):**
> *"The Bottler recognizes that the Company has entered into or may enter into agreements similar to this Agreement with other parties outside the Territory and **accepts the limitations such agreements may reasonably impose on the Bottler**."* **(L28)**

> *"The Bottler will not oppose any additional actions...even if such actions should limit the Bottler's rights under this Agreement."* **(L29)**

**Assessment:** CCBA formally acknowledges and accepts restrictions arising from parallel bottler agreements across COMESA. This moves the restriction from a simple bilateral vertical restraint toward a **network of parallel agreements with a coordinated effect** — which the CCC may treat as a concerted practice under Article 16, carrying higher legal exposure than a standard vertical restraint.

### 2.5 THE CRITICAL CLAUSE — Regulatory Termination Without Damages

**NBA Clause 25(a)(2) — Force Majeure / Regulatory (L86):**
> *"This Agreement may be terminated by the Company or the Bottler forthwith and **without liability for damages** by written notice given by the party entitled to terminate to the other party: if any part of this Agreement ceases to be in conformity with the laws or regulations applicable in the Territory and, as a result thereof, or as a result of any other laws affecting this Agreement, **any one of the material stipulations herein cannot be legally performed.**"*

**NBA Clause 34 — Severability (L126):**
> *"In the event of any provisions of this Agreement being or becoming legally ineffective or invalid, the validity or effect of the remaining provisions of this Agreement shall not be affected; provided that the invalidity or ineffectiveness of the said provisions shall not prevent or unduly hamper performance hereunder...The right to terminate in accordance with **Clause 25(a)(2) is not affected hereby.**"*

**Assessment — This is the existential risk:**

The territorial exclusivity provisions are the **single most material stipulation** of the franchise agreement. A COMESA ruling rendering them legally unenforceable triggers Clause 25(a)(2). Either TCCC or CCBA/CCHBC could then terminate the franchise agreement forthwith, without liability for damages.

The severability clause explicitly preserves this termination right — it is not cured by severability. CCHBC is paying $2.6bn for 75% of a company whose core franchise agreement is terminable by either party without damages if COMESA issues an adverse ruling.

This is not a "franchise gets modified" scenario. It is a "franchise terminates" scenario.

### 2.6 Fixed 10-Year Term — No Perpetual Franchise

**NBA Clause VI (L81–83):**
> *"This Agreement shall expire, without notice, on **10 years**, unless it has been earlier terminated...the Bottler shall have **no right to claim a tacit renewal**."*

> *"the Bottler may request an extension...If the Bottler has, in the **sole discretion of the Company**, satisfied the conditions...then the Company **may**...agree to extend."*

> *"At the expiration of any such additional term, this Agreement shall expire finally without further notice."*

**Assessment:** The franchise is not perpetual. It is a 10-year fixed term. Renewal is at TCCC's **sole discretion** with no guaranteed outcome. Any PPA or option valuation model assuming perpetual franchise duration is inconsistent with the contract. The contractually supportable useful life is **10 years certain**, with probability-weighted assumptions for extensions.

If COMESA forces clause amendments that TCCC finds commercially unacceptable, TCCC has a clean exit at the 10-year mark — no notice, no damages, no obligation.

### 2.7 Concentrate Pricing — Unilateral TCCC Discretion

**NBA Clause V (L75–76):**
> *"The Company reserves the right...to establish and to revise from time to time and at any time, **in its sole discretion**, the price of the Concentrate."*
> *"If the Bottler is unwilling to pay the revised price...this Agreement shall **terminate automatically without liability** by either party for damages three (3) calendar months after receipt of the Bottler's notification."*

**Assessment:** TCCC has unilateral pricing authority over the single most important input cost in CCBA's P&L. CCBA's only remedy if it disagrees with a price increase is to exit the franchise. This structural dependency is separate from the COMESA investigation but material to any DCF analysis of CCBA's franchise value.

### 2.8 No Disclosure to Regulatory Authorities Without TCCC Consent

**NBA Clause 34 (L121):**
> *"[The Bottler agrees] that it will make no representations or disclosures to public or government authorities or to any other third party, relating to the Concentrate, the Syrup or the Beverage **without the prior written consent of the Company**."*

**Assessment — three problems:**

1. **Likely unenforceable against COMESA:** Regulators have statutory investigatory powers; private contracts cannot prevent regulatory cooperation.
2. **May itself be a competition law concern:** A supplier contractually preventing a bottler from cooperating with competition authorities could be characterised as obstruction of regulatory proceedings — a standalone violation.
3. **Creates governance conflict post-close:** CCHBC (75%) and TCCC (25%) will have divergent interests in how CCBA engages with the COMESA investigation. This clause gives TCCC veto over CCBA's regulatory engagement — even as a 25% minority shareholder.

### 3.2 The 12-Month Antitrust Completion Cap — Exact Language

**Option Agreement Clause 3.2 — Call Option (L62):**
> *"The Option Completion Date shall be deemed to be extended for the reasonable time taken for making any application for a regulatory approval specified in Clause 3.1, and the time taken for obtaining such regulatory approval, **provided that the Option Completion Date shall in no event be later than twelve (12) months from the date of delivery of the relevant Call Option Exercise Notice.**"*

**Option Agreement Clause 6.2 — Put Option (L79):**
> Identical 12-month cap applies to Put Option exercises.

**Option Agreement Clause 3.4 — Lapse Trigger (L64):**
> *"If the conditions specified in Clause 3.1 have not been satisfied before the Option Completion Date (as extended pursuant to Clause 3.2, if applicable), or such later time as Olympus BV and Titan may agree in writing, **the relevant Call Option Exercise shall be deemed to have lapsed.**"*

**Assessment:** The 12-month cap is hard. If COMESA's antitrust approval for the option exercise is not obtained within 12 months of the exercise notice, that specific exercise lapses. The option itself is not extinguished (CCHBC can try again), but:
- The **Call Option Period** runs only to Year 5 from SPA completion (L59)
- If the main deal closes end 2026, the Call Option is live only until end 2031
- An exercise at Year 3 (end 2029) that lapses after 12 months leaves only ~1 year to re-exercise and complete
- A lapsed exercise is not a failed deal — but it compresses the remaining window and increases execution risk

For the **Put Option**, a lapse is more serious for TCCC: it has a defined window (Year 3.5–6, L454) and cannot extend beyond the sixth anniversary. A lapsed Put exercise wastes a portion of TCCC's exit window.

### 3.3 Option Antitrust Approval — What It Requires

**Option Agreement Clause 428 (definition):**
> *"'Option Antitrust Approval' means the approval of the clearance, approval or order by each Merger Control Authority in the Relevant Jurisdictions of the transactions contemplated by this Agreement."*

**Option Agreement Clause 457 (Relevant Jurisdictions):**
> *"means all jurisdictions where an antitrust and/or a merger control approval from a Governmental Entity will be required in connection with the transactions contemplated by the SPA, including the jurisdictions listed in Schedule 5 to the SPA."*

This includes COMESA. The option exercise therefore requires **a second COMESA merger review** — on top of the initial 75% acquisition review. If the COMESA conduct investigation is still live at the time of option exercise, the Commission will be reviewing the 25% step-up merger while simultaneously running an unresolved conduct case against TCCC regarding the franchise agreements of the very company being consolidated.

### 3.4 The Put Option Suspension — TCCC Could Be Trapped

**Option Agreement Clause 455 (Put Option Suspension Period definition):**
> *"means the period commencing on the date on which a legally binding order, writ, ruling, notice or similar decision of any Governmental Entity becomes effective which **makes the Put Option illegal or prohibits the consummation of the sale of the Option Shares** to Olympus BV pursuant to the Put Option."*

If COMESA issues an order (in either the conduct case or the merger review) that prohibits TCCC from exiting the remaining 25%, the Put Option is automatically suspended. TCCC could be locked as a 25% minority shareholder in a company it is trying to exit, with the suspension ending only when the Governmental Entity order ceases to be effective.

Given the Put Option has a hard outer limit of Year 6 from SPA completion (L76), a prolonged suspension could cause TCCC's put right to expire entirely.

### 3.5 Warranties — No Competition Law Cover

The Titan/TCCC warranties (Clauses 13–14) cover only:
- Corporate capacity, incorporation, authority
- Ownership and freedom from encumbrances of Option Shares
- No breach of applicable laws **in executing the option agreement itself**

There is **no warranty that:**
- The franchise agreements (NBA) comply with COMESA competition law
- There are no pending competition authority investigations into the franchise structure
- The territorial exclusivity provisions are legally enforceable in COMESA member states
- The franchise will remain on its current terms throughout the option period

The entire competition law risk on the franchise agreements sits with CCHBC with no contractual recourse against TCCC if the CCC finds against TCCC and franchise terms must be restructured.

## SECTION 4: REGULATORY RISK — IMPACT ON OPTION VALUATION

### 4.1 Assessment

The Option Agreement contains a hard 12-month cap on obtaining merger control approvals for the option exercise (Clause 3.2 / L62). If COMESA approval for the second-step 25% acquisition is not obtained within 12 months of the exercise notice, the exercise lapses. This is a real contractual mechanism.

**However, on full analysis, this risk is assessed as theoretical and not applied as a valuation adjustment.** The reasoning:

**1. The conduct case is likely to result in a minor amendment, not franchise impairment.**

COMESA's investigation targets passive sales restrictions in the franchise agreements. The expected outcome — consistent with the Heineken and Diageo precedents — is a minor amendment adding a passive sales carve-out. This does not affect CCBA's territorial exclusivity, brand rights, or core franchise economics. The underlying asset the option is written on is not materially impaired.

**2. TCCC has fully aligned economic incentives post-close.**

Post-close, TCCC is an indirect 25% owner of CCBA with a put option striking up at 2.75–4.10% annually. Blowing up the franchise — or allowing regulatory delay to cause the option exercise to lapse — directly destroys the value of their own structured exit. TCCC has every incentive to settle the conduct case quickly, amend the NBA in the minimum way necessary, and cooperate fully on obtaining option exercise approvals within the 12-month window.

**3. The second-step merger filing is expected to be routine.**

Once the conduct case is resolved (the most likely state by Year 3–5), the COMESA merger review of the option exercise is a standard step-up notification with no contested conduct overlay. There is no basis to assume COMESA would treat it as linked to a closed matter.

### 4.2 Acknowledgment for PPA Purposes

The theoretical risk is acknowledged in the PPA note as follows:

> *"The Option Agreement contains a 12-month cap on obtaining regulatory approvals for the option exercise (Clause 3.2), after which the exercise lapses. The COMESA Competition Commission has an active investigation into TCCC's bottler agreements (Case CCC/ACBP/A.22/09/02/2024, opened October 2024). Management has assessed this as a theoretical timing risk only: the investigation is expected to result in minor franchise amendments with no impact on CCBA's core franchise economics; TCCC's 25% post-close ownership creates fully aligned incentives to resolve the conduct case and cooperate on approvals; and the second-step merger filing is expected to be routine once the conduct case is settled. No valuation adjustment has been applied. The risk will be reassessed at each subsequent measurement date."*

## SECTION 6: GAPS AND RECOMMENDED ACTIONS

### 6.1 Items Not Reviewed — Further Diligence Required

1. **The Sale and Purchase Agreement (SPA):** The SPA is the primary document where MAC provisions, regulatory closing conditions, price adjustment mechanisms, and COMESA-specific representations should have been negotiated. The NBA and Option Agreement reviewed here confirm the risk is real — the SPA is where to assess how much protection CCHBC actually negotiated.

2. **CCBA subsidiary-level bottler agreements:** The NBA reviewed is the TCCC standard form (template). Each jurisdiction-specific agreement may have local law modifications. The COMESA member-state agreements (Kenya, Ethiopia, Uganda, Zambia, Eswatini, Malawi, Comoros) are the ones directly subject to the investigation.

3. **COMESA merger clearance conditions:** The terms on which COMESA ultimately clears the 75% acquisition will determine the baseline regulatory environment for the option exercise. Any conduct conditions imposed at merger clearance will directly affect P(Approval) for the option.

### 6.2 Contractual Remediation Priorities (for SPA negotiation if still open)

1. **Amend or remove Clause 25(a)(2) / L86** — or at minimum, add an indemnity from TCCC to CCHBC for any losses arising from a COMESA-triggered termination. This is the single most important contractual fix.
2. **Add passive sales carve-out to territorial restriction clauses** — bring the NBA into COMESA-compliant form before closing, removing the regulatory termination trigger proactively.
3. **Add COMESA competition law warranty** in the Option Agreement — TCCC should warrant that no ongoing competition authority investigations threaten the franchise structure.
4. **Negotiate COMESA conduct case resolution as a condition precedent** — or at minimum, require TCCC to use reasonable endeavours to resolve the conduct case before the option exercise window opens.
5. **Extend the 12-month cap or add re-exercise rights** — the 12-month cap on option completion is too tight given COMESA's established review timelines. Negotiate extension to 18–24 months, or add automatic re-exercise rights if a lapse occurs due solely to regulatory timing.
6. **Review and carve out L121** — the "no disclosure to government authorities" clause needs to be qualified to permit mandatory regulatory cooperation.
